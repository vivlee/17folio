Kolibri

Bringing Education Offline

Project Details

  • 2015 - 2016
  • 9 months
  • UX Design & Research

Based at the Qualcomm Institute Innovation Space, Foundation for Learning Equality is an organization dedicated to delivering education to underprivileged communities in need.

As a UX design intern at Learning Equality, I curated a library of UX resources and conducted my own user research leading up to the design of the coach experience on Learning Equality's newest educational platform, Kolibri.


Contributions as Intern Lead: Writing up user discussion guide, fielding user interviews over Google Hangouts, conducting remote usability testing, recording local ethnography, leading whiteboarding sessions, designing hi-fidelity screens, prototyping an interactive demo on InVision.

The Problem

  • Teaching Abroad

Novice Teacher: This is David‚Äôs first year teaching at a public school in Dubai.As a recent college graduate and fellow from Teach for America, David has been placed as an instructor in an underprivileged community in Dubai, India.


While he has limited experience as a teacher's assistant, David has very little formal training as a teacher. Eager to stay on track of his students' progress, however, he decides to utilize educational technology to help his teaching endeavors.


Except 60% of the world is without the Internet.

Coach Report Dashboard: Using a number of complex data visualizations, KA Lite's coach report dashboard is perfect for monitoring student effort, engagement, and mastery.

Background

  • Coach Report Dashboard

The previous coach report dashboard on Khan Academy Lite, Kolibri's predecessor, is a visual tool that provides usable information generated by individual student activity and offers insight to teachers and coaches about overall classroom performance.


After conducting contextual interviews with experienced users of Khan Academy Lite, I found that for some of our teachers in India specifically, coach reports are useful for:


Use Case 1: Identifying which concepts a student is struggling with through an individual progress report

Use Case 2: Monitoring activity in real-time to inform immediate changes to a teacher‚Äôs instruction

Use Case 3: Acting as an unambiguous alternative to gradebooks, giving teachers more focus and effective use of their time


With these resources, gathering data from global, offline communities was now a possibility.


User Discussion Guide: With the user discussion guide, we were able to keep user interviews structured and flexible.

Google Hangouts: Using Google Hangouts, we were able to interview users and conduct remote testing from all across the world.

User Research

  • User Interviews

Using a user discussion guide that I developed with ongoing team feedback, I was able to develop a user interview protocol that was both topic-based and demonstrative.


Flexible to individuals: I made sure to include a set of instructions for the exclusion of specific sections, depending on the level of familiarity that interview participants had with our software.

Can be used by everyone: With the extensive instructions included in the guide, we could either interview remote users over Google Hangouts, or we could have our partners interview users in our stead.


These features ensured that discussions within these interviews were flexible, insightful, and open to improvisation.


User Research

  • Usability Testing

Using our contacts as a well-recognized organization, we tapped into the educational community we grew from Learning Equality's previous product, Khan Academy Lite, to find teachers to interview.


I conducted a short task-based usability test over Google Hangouts to gauge a teacher's perspective on using coach reports. Using the think-aloud approach and a KA Lite dashboard generated with dummy student data, I gave each participant the same task to complete: determine the individual learning module that a struggling student is having trouble with.


Lack of Meaningful Indicators: Despite how easy to spot the color indicators were, they were not very informative as to how well students were doing in their assignments.

Too Much Information: In a classroom of more than twenty students, the vast amount of information offered almost immediately is very overwhelming to teachers.

Key Insights

  • Accurate & Actionable

Despite the gains that I mentioned earlier, big data on the coach report dashboard can be hard for even experienced teachers to digest, and friendlier data visualization will help KA Lite coaches better understand this information for everyday use.


Insight 1: Teachers sought more actionable feedback, in order to make more informed changes to their instruction.

Insight 2: They also wanted more accurate measures of student progress, in order to determine where students may be struggling.

Insight 3: Finally, teachers prioritized only the most relevant information, in order to engage students immediately and successfully.


As a result, my central goals for research and redesign were to: (1) identify meaningful markers for student struggle, improvement, or mastery, (2) prioritize specific methods of student engagement for our teachers and (3) provide actionable recommendations for their classroom practice.


Dynamic Grouping: From observing real classrooms, I found that rather than group students specifically by level of mastery, it was much more common to rotate students between different stations.

User Research

  • Local Ethnography

At Nativity Prep Academy, a teaching staff of 7 individuals manage the secondary education of around 65 low-income students from around the area. With our Implementations Lead, we made a day trip to the school in order to observe their staff meeting and interview some of the teachers at the school.


Here, I observed teachers in their habitual classroom routine, finding context in how they incorporated the virtual complexities of Khan Academy Lite into their hands-on curriculum.


Insight 1: Because the school population itself was so small, teachers often shared information about students over time and across classroom with each other about personal idiosyncrasies and successful engagement methods.

Insight 2: Most class instruction was hands-on and project-based, leaving very little attention to lessons that were purely theoretical.

Insight 3: Rather than assign struggling students in static and unchanging groups with other peers at a similar learning level, teachers often separated the classroom into different stations where students rotated every thirty minutes and thus, never stayed in the same instructional group for too long.


As a result, that practices in the classroom inherently were more malleable and interactive than we previously thought, providing us with the conclusion that we ultimately needed to focus on system flexibility and accessibility.

Affinity Diagram: I mapped my findings according to axes of familiarity with edtech and level of teaching experience.

User Research

  • Affinity Diagram

From the discussion notes gathered from my interviews, I was able to map my findings on an affinity diagram ‚Äîorganizing them on two axes of our participants' familiarity with digital tools and their level of teaching experience.


Henri (Persona 1): Inexperienced with educational technology, Henri needs help integrating digital tools into his teaching curriculum.

Kevin (Persona 2): As a novice teacher, Kevin has limited instructional experience and needs help targeting successful methods of engagement.

Mawar (Persona 3): As a volunteer instructor in her free time, Mawar is enthusiastic about her craft but needs organizational access to help.

Chandra (Persona 4): A seasoned professional with years of teaching experience under her belt, Chandra wants to retain a level of control with edtech.

User Research

  • Personas

From these discovered needs, I was able to come up with four personas that represented the various types of users involved.


From these personas, I was able to distill goals that were common to all these possible users for Kolibri's learning platform, which were to:

(1) Identify contributing factors to a student's struggle or mastery of the material.

(2) Determine successful methods of engagement and intervention for these behaviors.


Progress Reports: Inspired by Trello's simple, intuitive use of cards, we wanted to make academic topics within individual student reports easily selectable.

Widgets: In our second iteration the following week, we revisited the idea of including a dashboard with widgets and modules, in hope of providing a much broader roadmap for users.

Ideation Process

  • Wireframes

In order to brainstorm the various types of wireframes possible to solve for these needs, another UX intern and I met up with each other to whiteboard several ideas we had for redesigning the coach report experience.


Feature 1: An integrated lesson plan with actionable recommendations, as we found that although the original dashboard presented a large amount of information to use in planning classroom instruction, there was little guidance or direction on how this data could be interpreted

Feature 2: Progress statistics for weekly class development, in order track student improvement on an ongoing basis.

Feature 3: A separate module for peer-to-peer group planning, because many of our interview participants organized student engagement in dynamic groups rather than single out individual intervention.


With data gathered from our user interviews, we prioritized adding these features above anything else.


Final Designs

  • Hi-fidelity Prototyping

After updating the personas, I used Sketch 3 to translate our designs into early screens for a high fidelity prototype.


Unfortunately, the recent designs for the coach experience have changed since then, but I can't reveal my final contributions to this project due to it being under wraps.

An Incredible Learning Experience: Despite how fun it was working in a smaller, more intimate startup, I definitely learned a lot by being able to take up multiple design roles.

Final Thoughts

  • Designing at a Startup

As my first UX design internship, I made a lot of progress in the shaky transition from academic assignments to industry-appropriate standards. It was difficult designing for instructors when I had little teaching experience myself, but being on the other side of the school system lended me empathetic views that allowed me to account for the entire classroom as a context. It inspired me to dig deep into my research, and I was able to explore forays into remote usability testing, contextual interviews, and ethnographic fieldwork.


My biggest contributions were my methods for user research. With the user discussion guide I developed, the organization could now obtain richer, qualitative feedback that would otherwise be lost in a regular questionnaire or survey. Even after my exit from the internship, the protocol that I detailed myself has still been used to gather further research by the remaining full-timers on the team.
