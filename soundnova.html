Soundnova

Keeping Your Music on Trend

Project Details

  • Fall 2016
  • 2 weeks
  • Interaction Design
  • View Prototype →

As a final project for my class on information architecture, my team and I were tasked to redesign a major streaming application to best support music management and discovery.

Ultimately, we chose to design an app with extensive playlist capabilities, as we found through extensive research and competitive analysis that a user’s ability to create, revise, and update playlists on current platforms were extremely limited.


Contributions as Design Lead: Conducting contextual interviews, creating the sitemaps and competitive analysis, designing all the wireframes and workflows for our app, mocking up the hi-fidelity screens using Sketch 3, and uploading them onto InVision.

Increased Music Discovery: A separate discovery tab for exploring popular music allows users to find tracks, albums, and artists trending by the number of plays.

Smarter Playlist Management: With added filters, it is now easy to determine which songs users have enjoyed listening to and have appreciated the most over time.

The Problem

  • Cleaning the Music Library

Natalie is a college student who enjoys making playlists for going on road trips, working out, and doing homework. Because she has so many occasions in which she creates these playlists, she rarely has time to clean up or revise her existing ones. At this point, her music library has become bloated, and she finds herself either making an entirely new playlist (thus, further cluttering up her library) or revising her current playlists (potentially losing important tracks).

Interview Questions: When forming our interview questions, we focused on identifying key use cases and scenarios they would encounter, as well as potential frustrations that could appear.

Contextual Interviews: In order to discover design opportunities, I interviewed 9 UC San Diego undergraduates, from which we were able to extract 31 use cases —19 of which were unique.

User Research

  • Contextual Interviews

Ultimately, our goal was to design a user experience that better supported the tasks of creating, managing, and revising music playlists. We were interested in leveraging a user’s existing habits in these scenarios, driving us to conduct contextual interviews where we not only expanded on the routine of an individual’s experience with playlists —but also observed their interactions with these applications in real-time.


We asked ourselves: In what scenarios did users typically create, add to, and manage their playlists? What goals did they want to accomplish, and what frustrations were commonplace?


By probing our participants for these answers and asking them to walk us through their normal routine, we were able to gain better context on some of the painpoints centered around playlist management.


Key Insights

After our contextual inquiry, my team and I got back together to discuss our findings.


Insight 1: While most playlists were created for personal use, there were various instances where playlists were shared among a number of individuals, such as during road trips, parties, and hangouts.

Insight 2: When discovering new music genres or categories for the first time, users often began with influential artists that were popular or 'trending' for that genre.

Insight 3: When revising or updating their playlists, interview participants tended to remove songs that they skipped often first.


As a result, we decided to center our redesign around two main use cases: (1) creating playlists with the intention to discover new genres, and (2) updating existing playlists with a person's changing taste in music.


Spotify: When asking participants to add a new song to their playlists, I saw that it was easy to browse Spotify by genre and mood.

SoundCloud: To add a new song to their existing playlists, all users had to do on SoundCloud was open the related actions menu.

User Research

  • Competitive Analysis

In order to gauge the overall usability of existing streaming applications, I performed a competitive task analysis of Spotify, YouTube, and SoundCloud —all of which were music applications mentioned by our 9 interview participants.


I also studied the information architecture of each application by charting the first, second, and third level navigation of particular playlist features in separate sitemaps.


Spotify Sitemap: Spotify allows for music discoverability based on genre and mood, which can be accessed through the 'Browse'.

YouTube Sitemap: YouTube makes explicit distinctions between channels that a user is subscribed to and those that are trending.

SoundCloud Sitemap: SoundCloud's opportunity for music discoverability is deeply hidden under the 'Search' navigation.

Information Architecture

  • Sitemaps

Despite meeting with six different teams, my product manager and I decided to narrow down our use case to a single focus: making changes in budget and finances.


In order to pinpoint the main goals, frustrations, and behaviors that someone for this use case would face, I created a persona based on our interview findings to organize early ideas for how to structure the waterfall chart: focusing on Sara, an accountant at a medium-sized company in California that's doing well to break even —but has the potential to become more profitable.


As a financial analyst, Sara's main goal is to determine which areas her company makes the largest gains and losses, and make cuts to items that will ensure the company makes a marginal profit.


Poor Trending Discoverability: In this screenshot, there’s no intuitive way to reach popular music charts in SoundCloud’s original design. Instead, we have to navigate to the ‘Search’ screen, where we must scroll towards the bottom of the page in order to find these categories.

No Playlist Renaming or Track Filtering: Soundcloud's edit playlist overflow menu had no options for renaming the playlist or for filtering the tracks. In order to revise playlists, users had to go through each track themselves and consciously decide whether to keep it in the list.

Key Findings

  • Discovery & Decision-making

For several scenarios, we compared experiences that we considered ‘good’ to experiences that we labeled as ‘less good.'


From our interviews, participants claimed that initial ventures into unfamiliar music genres began with listening to the most popular artists in that domain, rather than with less known, obscure artists.


On SoundCloud, however, these entry points for music discovery were hidden under the unintuitive 'Search' navigation.


We also found from our contextual inquiry that users were reluctant to go back and revise their playlists due to the amount of decision-making that had to be made on whether to delete a song or not.


Those that did update their playlists on a regular basis, in fact, took into account how often they listened to each track. However, SoundCloud's platform allowed almost little to no control over a user's personal playlists.


Therefore, our key design decisions for creating a new playlist app involved: (1) including an accessible navigation for new, trending music, and (2) creating a intuitive, more seamless way to decide whether to keep a song in a playlist.

Add to Playlist Workflow: In the adding workflow, users can either add a track they've discovered from the trending page or add music that they've found through the search navigation.

Revising Playlist Workflow: In the revising workflow, users can filter by the least and the most played tracks in the library, to revise playlists according to what songs they skip or listen to the most.

Information Architecture

  • Wireframes & Workflows

As the interaction design lead of my team, I created the workflows for (1) adding a new track to an existing playlist and (2) revising an existing playlist in the library.


From our contextual interviews using SoundCloud, we found that music discovery must be made explicit through either a trending feed separate from the home feed or through an accessible search function.


Users claimed that when creating a playlist, they were more interested in finding recommendations that were considered 'trendy' and popular to the public when taking new forays into music, rather than rely purely on suggested tracks from artists they have already heard of.


As a result, I prioritized being able to easily discover (and then, add) new tracks to a user's playlists.


From our contextual interviews using Spotify, we also saw that users made gratuitous use of the filters and sorting options on its application.


When asked to walk through how they revised their existing playlists, they replied that they often removed songs they didn't listen to often or tended to skip.


Therefore, I specifically designed the ability to filter by the number of plays, in order to allow users to quickly revise and update playlists for songs they are no longer interested in.


Explicit Music Discovery: For more transparent exploration, there are two entry points for music discovery: the trending music page and through the application's search function.

Adding to Playlist: After selecting a track that they may be interested in, users can add the track to an existing playlist in their library.

Playlist Revision: By sorting these tracks by the number of plays, users can determine which tracks they listen to the most or least, helping decide which ones to remove or keep.

Final Thoughts

  • Designing a Mobile App

Ultimately, I gained a lot of insight from trying to design an app based on some of the most popular music platforms, specifically in how we can turn visible breakdowns into concrete design opportunities for a better user experience. My weaknesses lie in practicing design with a critical eye, and from this assignment, I’ve become sharper and more steadfast in creating refined product experiences for a user’s primary needs.


Thinking back, while it was important to make the distinction between popular plays and personal plays, there's also the factor of how old a song is, which can create problems in determining the number of plays for trending tracks. In the future, I would love to explore more time-sensitive filters and conditions for how to appropriately flag tracks that are 'currently in'.
