Froglanders Café

Designing Food for Web and Mobile

Project Details

  • Winter 2017
  • 10 weeks
  • Responsive Web Design
  • View Website →

As part of a quarter-long project for my practicum in pro web design, I worked in a team of 4 to find a client in need of a new website, meeting with him every 2 weeks to account for constant communication and feedback. As a studio-based class, I actively participated in presentations and design critique sessions with my peers.


As a result, my team of 4 delivered a fully functional, HTML/CSS prototype of our website design For Froglanders Café, a frozen yogurt shop in La Jolla.


Contributions as Designer & Developer: Writing up questions, conducting user interviews, developing personas, creating scenarios and use cases, contributing to competitive analysis and moodboards, mocking up wireframes, leading visual design guidelines, contributing to front-end development.

Branding: Tasked with branding Froglanders Cafe's website, we opted for a careful mix of playful and trendy to create a friendly image.

Today's Flavors: In a one-page website, we focused on displaying today's available flavors, saving users time to look for what they need.

How It Works: With an emphasis on healthy, natural ingredients, a section on how frozen yogurt is made informs users of its freshness.

Client Information

  • Overview

While searching in the local community, my team and I stressed finding client opportunities off-campus, as we thought this was the perfect chance to apply industry-level practices outside of the classroom. While we briefly considered contacting several coffee shops downtown, we ultimately decided to work with Froglanders Café.


Froglanders Frozen Yogurt Café: Based in La Jolla, this family-owned frozen yogurt shop is about a 15 minutes’ drive from UC San Diego.


After speaking with our client Shah, whose café has been in business for 31 years, we found that their most popular user demographic included families and their least popular demographic was made up of college students.

Participatory Design: In our session of participatory design, I had our client mark his previous website with opportunities for redesign.

Client Needs & Objectives

  • Minimum Requirements

Initial Req's: In order to gather our initial requirements for our redesign, a thorough discussion with our client gave us a better idea on how to go about meeting his business goals.


Client Survey: To do this, I first probed the client with questions my teammates and I prepared before the meeting. Asking questions, such as his inspiration for his cafe and what makes it different from other frozen yogurt shops, helped us understand the vision he has for his business.

Participatory Design: After I collected his responses, I invited him to draw out how he thought the content should be arranged on his website. We also printed out pages of his original site in the early 2000s, prompting him to mark typical user behavior on the site, identify key opportunities for change, and any personal causes for concern.


From there, we were able to gain a better idea of the goals and objectives that our client had in mind.


Goals & Objectives: After some discussion, we prioritized making the website mobile-first, image-heavy, and informative.

Key Goals

  • Mobile-First Approach

61% of users are unlikely to return to a mobile site they had trouble accessing and 40% visit a competitor’s site instead: After the client meeting, the four of us met to identify our key goals and discuss our top 10 project priorities for the quarter.


Among 10 objectives, we placed greater weight on designing a website that was: mobile-friendly, image-heavy, and informative.

Contextual Interviews: In our contextual interviews, I asked users to walk me through their typical routine of finding new places to eat at.

My Findings: From our user interviews, I was able to distill key insights about how parents and college students find places to eat.

User Research

  • User Interviews

Interviewing parents and college students: In order to gather information on the website’s audience, we interviewed a total of 8 parents and 8 college students (4 frequent go-ers and 4 infrequent).


Questions: In addition to asking specific questions about their goals and needs in their demographic, we also asked more general questions on how they used their devices to research new places to eat.


Once we consolidated our user data, I built user groups in order to understand our users. Within these groups, I included personas, use cases, and user scenarios.


Parent Persona: From our user interviews, we found that parents often used desserts as a reward for their kids' good behavior.

Understanding the motivations and needs of parents: We found that parents often used desserts as a reward for good behavior.


Insight: A lot of parents look for a delicious and healthy alternative, and thus would use the website to learn more about Froglanders' natural ingredients and processes.


—Therefore, our main goal to target parents is: provide detailed information on nutrition and natural ingredients.

Competitive Analysis

  • Image-heavy vs. Text-heavy

Before we started designing the website, we analyzed what local/national competitors in the food industry used for their websites. We looked closely at what worked and what didn't work in terms of branding, content, site architecture, and more.


Several competitors we looked at included Hammond’s, Yogurtland, Pinkberry, and Bing Haus. Common trends we found include:


Strong Imagery: Rather than rely on text to convey meaningful values of people eating food, 3 out of 4 competitors use heavy imagery to illustrate desserts as an experience to be had, rather than a product to be bought.

Ubiquitous Branding: Furthermore, strong consistency between typography and color make users associate a strong, welcoming brand for these competitors.


From there, we were able to focus on what we could consider taking inspiration from in each website.


Playful Moodboard: To explore a fun and playful mood, we compiled bright images that were vivid in color, fueled by nostalgia.

Trendy Moodboard: To explore a trendy, more modern mood, we chose textures that were more mature and rustic, emphasizing on ambience.

Moodboards

  • Fun vs. Café

Because the words ‘dessert’ and ‘café’ can bring up incredibly different images in our head, I wanted to explore two vastly different approaches for our mood boards —rather than settle on just one potential ambience.


We eventually diverged into two moodboards: one to convey a fun and playful mood, and the other to articulate a trendy, more minimalistic approach.

For the playful mood, we used bright colors and vivid imagery to represent the fun and excitement of being a child, while for the trendy mood, we explored geometric shapes and minimalistic layouts to symbolize a clean, hipster feeling.

Feedback: From meeting with our client, we received positive feedback on the competitive analysis we compiled. Ultimately, we were able to take away several ideas from studying his competitors.

The Sweet Spot: Although we explored two seemingly separate brands of playful and trendy, employing a combination of the two can result in a wholesome, welcoming image.

Client Feedback

  • Fun & Café = Trendy

From meeting with our client, we found that rather than focus on one end or the other, incorporating elements from both the fun moodboard and the trendy moodboard was the best option for conveying Froglanders’ uniqueness.


After much deliberation, we realized that the sweet spot between these domains of fun and trendy —would be a brand that was welcoming, playful enough to attract families and friendly enough to appeal to college students.

Mobile Wireframes: In our initial mobile wireframes, we took into account how little room there was for everything, and prioritized including only the most relevant information needed for use cases.

Desktop Wireframes: In our desktop wireframes, we wanted to take advantage of the increased real estate, using this change to emphasize large, high-quality imagery.

Wireframing

  • Lo-fidelity Designs

From our user interviews and our client feedback, we mocked up lo-fidelity wireframes to explore the basic mobile and web layouts our team could use for the final design.


Each of us sketched out our version of the Froglanders website.


This was to promote divergent thinking in order for more creative outputs and a larger pool of ideas to choose from. Afterwards, we did a critique session, giving feedback on each other's sketch before we consolidated our ideas into a single set of wireframes.

Sitemap: Through the homepage, users can also access the about page, information about the store, the flavor menu, how frozen yogurt is made, and contact information.

Key Features

  • Detailed Information

I further annotated these screens with functional specs, to scope out potential technicalities our team may run into when implementing the site using HTML, CSS, and Javascript.


Main specifications include: A responsive slider for featured items (in order to save limited mobile real estate), a filterable flavor gallery, and an integrated Google Maps API.

Lookback: After prototyping our wireframes on InVision, we conducted user testing on how people would interact on desktop and mobile.

User Testing

  • Task-based Analysis

In order to test our prior assumptions about our user groups, we looked towards task-based analysis to validate our hypotheses.


Parent Goal: View nutrients (From user interviews, we saw that one of their main goals included finding a healthy reward for their kids.)


Student Goal: Find hours (From user interviews, we noted that college students were more concerned about the flexibility of hours at an establishment)


From our assumptions, we predicted that detailed, nutritional information would help health-conscious parents and students decide whether they want to go to Froglanders Café.

We also thought that users would want to know the hours and address at the very bottom, after they’ve made their decision to go after viewing the rest of the website.

Results

  • 'Less is More'

To do this, we combined usage of Lookback.io to record our user testing, using it to study second-by-second engagement of the wireframes.


Insight: However, from our findings, we saw that more information was not necessarily better.


In fact, despite displaying all available flavors on the menu in an effort to be detailed and informative, users only wanted to see the most immediately relevant information first.


From previous interviews: When researching possible frozen yogurt flavors, users (both students and parents alike) were more concerned with today’s flavors, rather than all possible rotations.


Therefore, providing a menu for all possible flavors made this task much more tedious, especially when users are prioritizing speed and efficiency.

This is further obstructed by the fact that in an effort to provide all ingredients in a flavor, we made accessibility to nutritional information too dense and bloated for real-world use cases that might only be a matter of needing to know whether something was sugar-free or lactose-free.

Results

  • 'Immediate and Relevant'

Additionally, while it was easy to navigate to the hours at the bottom of the page, displaying all available hours of the week was not necessarily helpful in achieving the task.


From previous interviews: We found that although students can make plans when to go out several weeks in advance, they don’t often choose plans for where until the last minute.


More concerned with whether a place would be open at the moment they’re looking it up, this task was made more difficult by having to search through all hours.


Branding Guidelines: For our branding guidelines, we incorporated colors that were vivid, but not too gaudy, and used the font Amatic SC to highlight the friendly, playful nature of Froglanders.

Photoshoot In Session: To abide by our branding strategy, we also took all the pictures we needed for the website design (thanks to my talented teammate Natalie!)

Branding

  • Friendly

Friendly: From our earlier feedback, our team decided on presenting a website that conveyed a friendly, welcoming brand to users. As a result, we worked on creating a logo that was playful and nostalgic. Similar to a vintage stamp, the logo highlights the symbolic frog that is the mascot of the cafe and gives a happy feel.

Furthermore, we chose typography and colors that were lively and bright, deciding on a more curly, hand-written font in lieu of a typeface that was more modern and straightforward.

Client vs. Audience Objectives: Despite appearing to be from two entirely different motivations, comparing the objectives of the client vs. the audience revealed some very important overlaps.

Clients vs. Audience Needs

  • How are they related?

As we began closing in on our final product, our team took time to revisit our main goals and objectives, where I wrote up a comparison between our client goals and audience goals in our creative brief.


Communication of Products ⇿ Easy-to-Read Menu: Because the client’s shop rotates their frozen yogurt flavors daily, in addition to offering more than 75+ toppings, the extensive menu must be easy-to-read. From our user testing, we found that this essentially meant easy-to-skim, as users often have limited time to browse for what they want.


Online Branding ⇿ Shop Atmosphere: In order to properly convey the frozen yogurt shop’s cafe-like atmosphere, online branding presence is important and must express this image throughout the site. When these two are connected, the audience should be able to effectively gauge Froglanders’ in-store atmosphere just from browsing the site.


History & Core Values ⇿ Rich Imagery: Like we saw from our earlier competitive analysis, images can often express more than words. Because most of the staff is made up of the client and his parents, we want to leverage this business image as historic, personable, and full of character —with the use of rich, vibrant images.


Increase in New Customers ⇿ Easy Navigation: With goals for a 20% increase in walk-in traffic, the website must then be intuitive and straightforward enough for new users to easily navigate.


While these two sets of goals initially appeared to be unrelated, closer examination reveals that in fact, there are many overlaps between what the client needed and what the website audience needed.


Final Design: With additional input from our client and from our findings in user testing, we finally opted to develop a one-page website.

Final Design

  • Key Decisions

For our final design, we revisited our main objectives and prioritized a site that was mobile-friendly, image-heavy, and informative.


However, from user testing, we found that more information is not necessarily better: As a result, for our final design, I cut back on words and instead chose images that would convey Froglanders’ values for character, service and natural delight.


Although we tried showing all flavors at the top —separate from the other flavors— we made the final decision to exclude flavors that weren’t available that day. As for nutritional information, we simplified the previous list of ingredients into categories such as sugar-free, lactose-free, and vegan, indicated by labels below a given flavor.


Furthermore, users only seek the most immediately relevant information in their goals For our final design, I included a (dismissible) banner that would follow users as they scroll, informing them of that day’s hours from the very beginning.


Although I had to change the verbiage around, I ultimately decided to not only display whether the cafe was open at the time of viewing —but also when it would be open until on that day.


Finally, in order to craft a smooth narrative in conjunction with Froglander Cafe’s brand: The page opens with an introductory header image, followed by an informative, personal blurb about its history.


Below that, frozen yogurt flavors and toppings can be read, as well as a process describing the natural ingredients used in its desserts. At the very bottom, the hours and address can be found.

Final Thoughts

  • Designing for a Client

I’m happy to say that I was able to finish off my last design class at UCSD with a bang. I had an incredible team working with my team and my client to design a responsive site that would meet the needs for both families in San Diego, as well as local college students. Despite my previous experience working with a client at the Museum of Photographic Arts, I learned a lot about balancing my client’s needs with the audience’s needs —especially in my role as a UX designer and user advocate.


From the design point of view, the target audience's objectives in using the finished program are far more important than the client's objectives, and the difference must be kept straight. However, comparing the two can allow us to draw parallels that might not otherwise be seen.


One of the hardest decisions we made as a team included reworking our initial multi-page design into a one-page website, due to our findings from later testing. Sometimes, our first assumptions are wrong —and it’s critical that despite conducting user interviews and developing personas to define these assumptions, we ultimately need to test the credibility of our hypotheses by putting them in front of real people.
