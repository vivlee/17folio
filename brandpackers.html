Brandpackers

Humanizing University Recruitment

Project Details

  • Spring 2016
  • 10 weeks
  • Startup Design
  • View Prototype →

For my class on interaction design, I was tasked with developing a startup project based on the current trends of the world in 2016.


In an effort to solve the disparity in recruitment between big companies and small companies in tech, we designed an organic way for publicity to run in campus communities —by capitalizing on the personal, professional, and academic connections of university students.


Contributions as Design Lead: Directing ideation sessions, writing up all online surveys, conducting interviews and service enactments, designing the high-fidelity screens, and prototyping an interactive workflow.

Get Matched: Our online service helps companies connect with these campus ambassadors by matching their needs with the skills and talent to grow their brand presence.

Reach Far: By taking advantage of our students’ personal, professional, and academic networks, a company's brand identity can spread through wider campus communities.

Meet Recruiting Success: With our detailed follow-up reports, our ambassadors are dedicated to delivering quality events for companies' long-term brand success.

The Problem

  • University Recruitment is Flawed

Providing professional opportunities: Kevin is a 2nd year Computer Science major. With limited time at the career fair, Kevin ultimately decides to talk to the Big 4, overlooking smaller companies at the venue that would have otherwise been a perfect fit. However, he soon gets rejected to all of them and feels extremely discouraged.


At this point in time, he has no idea what to do and has no internship lined up this summer.


Hiring top student talent: Biotek is a medium-sized analytics company based in San Diego. It doesn’t have the reputation or brand of companies like Facebook and Google, but Illumia is looking to offer an internship program to invest in young talent. Interested in hiring the best interns possible, the company offers fair compensation and a great environment for growth.


Despite their best recruiting efforts, they only receive minimal interest at the career fair.

Affinity Diagram: By mapping out the potential problems we found in political, economic, social, and technological spaces, we narrowed down our opportunity to the university recruiting landscape.

User Research

  • Contextual Interviews

Understanding the world in 2016: In order to discover the needs of the world in 2016, my team and I did research on emerging issues in the recent year, including the changing state of the economy, the growing public interest in AI/VR, and the unemployment landscape.


We asked ourselves: What problems were populations of today facing? Where was technology heading, and what were obstacles to these goals?


To fully explore the possibilities in every domain, the four of us also did a PEST analysis on current issues in the U.S.


Value Flow Model: In this value flow model, we see that the university recruiting system involves many, complex connections between students, recruiters, and the university itself.

Key Insights

  • Value Flow Model

We realized that there was a strong need for more personalized job-candidate matching: Not only were students having trouble spreading out their internship applications evenly across the tech industry —but company recruiters were also facing massive challenges in finding a good fit for their job positions.


With these three stakeholders in mind, my team and I made a value flow model of the relationships involved in this system.

Jobseeker Survey: In this Google form, I asked questions such as students' academic year, their past experience, and the skills they had for positions they were looking for.

Recruiter Survey: In this Google form, I asked questions about their company statistics, what skills and background they were looking for, and what were main recruiting challenges.

User Research

  • Online Surveys

Understanding the needs of both companies and students: In order to further scope interest from students and companies, I used social media outlets on Linkedin, Twitter, and Facebook to send out two different Google Forms for both students and for recruiters. After a couple of days, we received a grand total of 97 student responses and 6 recruiter responses.


Insight 1: From our responses, we discovered that most students started their internship search from the top, where jobs are the fewest and where competition is especially fierce.


Often, university students started by applying to the biggest industry names first.


Insight 2: And when we asked recruiters what their biggest reason for applicant rejection was, 83.3% of respondents rated ‘poor match between candidate and job position’ as their biggest reason for rejection!


—And 100% of recruiters named ‘finding good candidates’ as their organization’s biggest challenge!


From these responses, we saw two different —but related— user perspectives to this design problem of recruitment.

Key Findings

  • How Might We Questions

From our ventures in user research, we were able to define our main challenges.


Problem 1: Finding jobs that they qualify for, given their current skills and experience.

Problem 2: Increasing the number of applicants who are interested in the opportunities they have to offer.


So how might we...

(1) Help students seize learning opportunities in the tech industry —without overlooking smaller, lesser known companies?

(2) Help recruiters find top student talent —without also losing these applicants to bigger companies with more social presence?

Group Brainstorming: Together with our studio peers, we took part in group brainstorming, where we teamed up to mass-generate solutions to our potential design opportunities.

Rapid Ideation: This ultimately resulted in rapid ideation within 5-minute design sprints, allowing our team to explore more than 50 solutions over the course of one session.

User Research

  • Ideation Sessions

In order to come up with the early concepts for our startup, we were paired off with our studio peers to brainstorm and mass-generate multiple solutions from our findings.


With over 50+ solutions generated, favorite concepts from this design jam included:

(1) An online platform for companies to host campus hack-a-thon events

(2) A competition board for companies to post real problems that students can solve

(3) A campus ambassador program to get quick access to PR at top schools

Where to Apply Next?: Students related the most to Kevin, who is initially rejected from Big 4 and later receives an offer from a smaller company in the local area.

  • Storyboards & Scenarios

In order to better contextualize the various ideas we came up with, my teammates and I drew six storyboards that represented scenarios faced by both recruiters and university students. I drew four of these storyboards, including 3 for students and 1 for recruiters.

User Research

  • Speed Dating Sessions

Afterwards, I conducted a series of speed-dating sessions with our storyboards, rapidly showing these scenarios to potential stakeholders and asking which ones they could relate most strongly to.


For the most part, students resonated with the struggles of not knowing where to apply.


After applying to biggest names in the industry (i.e. Big 4), students had the most trouble thinking of which companies to try next.


Students I interviewed claimed that they just listed out all the companies that they knew off the top of their head, and applied to all relevant and available positions —even if they weren’t what they wanted to apply to.


But most importantly, students said that...


"I needed experience, so I reached out to some of my friends."

—One student describing his outreach for personal connections to gain experience.


"I wasn’t sure where to apply and asked around to my friends."

—One student solving his uncertainty by tapping into information from his friends.


From my speed dating sessions, I found that many of the people I talked to had friends in the same field that often gave them the professional opportunities they were seeking.

Revised Value Flow Model: From our speed dating sessions and user interviews, we found a potential new connection for matching jobs that was much more personal and universal — student ambassadors.

Key Findings

  • Value Flow Model

Despite our previous assumptions, student who were successful in securing a position did not rely on their skills and academic resources to find their internships.


Instead, they capitalized on their personal network to reach out to people they knew who would give them the information and professional connections they needed.


So how do we design something students can trust?


Student Profile Cards: These student profile cards included information on organizations these imaginary ambassadors were a part of, their specialties/skills, and their major.

Social Media Template: In an effort to ensure that company images were properly represented by campus ambassadors, we utilized the idea of a 'template' that companies could provide for students to post.

User Research

  • Service Enactments

In order to test this risk of how companies can ensure their image is being accurately represented by student ambassadors, Jessica and I worked together to complete service enactments with 2 companies and 1 student.


In fact, due to the number of interest expressed by our participants, we were able to indirectly involve multiple individuals in a realistic system that would take place in a brand-ambassador relationship.


First, I interviewed 2 San Diego-based startups that I recruited using my own personal and professional connections.


In order to simulate what it would be like to pick out brand ambassadors from a directory, I gave these startups a quick on-boarding slide deck to explain to them the company model and a series of student profile cards for our companies to choose from, both of which I created myself on Google Slides.


Then, I interviewed a random student that my teammate recruited from UCSD’s Facebook group.


After posting on our university’s Free and For Sale group advertising a one-time brand ambassador stint, my team member received a response from Karen, a 3rd year Communications major with 2400+ Facebook friends, a member of the Tri Delta sorority, and a residential assistant for the dorms.


After we both met with her, we gave her a brief on-boarding document with company details, as well as a template for how she should post to social media —which I was able to create using real details we received from the social app startup we interviewed earlier.

Recruiter: The recruiter I interviewed explains how he would much rather have job candidates who were qualified and genuinely interested in company opportunities.

Ambassador: Our 'ambassador' explains how she decides which groups to post in, revealing why she has decided to post the job listing in her social sorority group.

Key Findings

  • Service Enactments

Companies don't want just anyone: Both the CFO and the recruiter from the two companies we interviewed did not want ambassadors to be tabling on Library Walk, asserting that:


"People on Library Walk are totally random people who haven’t done research on our company yet."

— Recruiter on wanting more targeted student recruitment.


Instead, they strongly preferred brand ambassadors who posted on certain social media groups, ambassadors who could make announcements before specific lectures, and those who could reach out to particular student organizations.


We also found that students follow patterns when spreading information: In total, we watched Karen post in over 25 Facebook groups in an hour. Starting with personal networks, then moving onto academic connections —and finally, returning back to her personal networks.


But most importantly, when we asked her how she chose which groups to post in, she says she decides by picking:


"What I’m active in, where I’m a leader in, where I have already established a community."

— Student ambassador on personalized social media posting.

Social Media Success: Within a couple of days, having the ambassador post on social media resulted in 500+ clicks.

Open Job Listing: As a result of this small pilot test, the company we interviewed received over 500+ hits in only a couple of days.

Final Results

  • Service Enactments

Using Google’s URL Shortener, I was able to track activity of the job listing that our ambassador posted over the course of a weekend.


Posting received 521 total clicks over 3 days: After posting in 26 groups, this social media campaign was extremely successful in attracting campus interest to this new startup.


At least 5 applicants have e-mailed the company: After that weekend, 3 job interviews were scheduled with the startup within a few days, bringing in more qualified candidates.


Clearly, students have connections.


—And from what we see, employing more targeted marketing by capitalizing on a student’s personal, professional, and academic connections can ultimately result in more focused student interest for lesser known tech companies.

Visual Design

  • Branding

In order to explore the visual possibilities for our startup values, my teammates and I created a mood board of our brand ideals based on the impressions we wanted our users to have.


We wanted to portray our brand as trustworthy, familiar, recognized, welcoming, and authentic —to describe the honest values of our startup mission.


We also wanted to paint ourselves as community, presence, leadership, and confidence —to show our ambassadors’ strong representation of companies.


As the leading product designer of the team, I also explored several typeface and visuals for our startup logo, ultimately deciding on combinations of Panton’s strong, bold nature and an edgy backpack visual for the final image.

Account Registration: Users of Brandpackers can sign up for either an ambassador account or company account, allowing for seamless communication between both parties.

Dashboard View: Company users can view ongoing progress of their campaigns on the dashboard, while students can view forums for event ideas and company communication.

Campaign Creation: By selecting campaign packages, companies can buy social media posts, info sessions, tabling, or any combination of these events.

Final Thoughts

  • Designing a Startup

Ultimately, I gained a lot of insight from trying to design an app based on some of the most popular music platforms, specifically in how we can turn visible breakdowns into concrete design opportunities for a better user experience. My weaknesses lie in practicing design with a critical eye, and from this assignment, I’ve become sharper and more steadfast in creating refined product experiences for a user’s primary needs.


Thinking back, while it was important to make the distinction between popular plays and personal plays, there's also the factor of how old a song is, which can create problems in determining the number of plays for trending tracks. In the future, I would love to explore more time-sensitive filters and conditions for how to appropriately flag tracks that are 'currently in'.
