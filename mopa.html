MOPA

Rethinking Family Experiences

Project Details

  • Spring 2016
  • 10 weeks
  • AR Design

For our quarter long cognitive design studio, we worked with our client at the Museum of Photographic Arts, a local institution in Balboa Park dedicated to collecting and preserving the history of photography.

For the course of three months, our team worked with our client to fully reimagine how we can actively engage family visitors, who make up some of the lowest demographics in attendance this year.


Contributions as Designer: Documenting fieldwork (notes, photography), leading concept ideation sessions, parallel prototyping various solutions, mocking up our final hi-fidelity designs.

Interact with exhibits in new ways: A downloadable augmented reality app allows Kate and her daughter, Emily, to explore the gallery through active collaboration, puzzle-solving, and megacognition.

Take home this playful souvenir: After their visit, Emily can print the completed activities, and the two of them talk about their experience in the car ride home.

The result: With MOPA's augmented reality app, Kate and her daughter are now more informed about photography, are motivated to visit the museum again, and ultimately have become closer as a family.

The Problem

  • A More Engaging Family Experience

Kate is a 34-year-old single parent who wants to spend more time with her daughter. Exhausted from working long hours during the week, it is often difficult for her to connect with her daughter on a more personal level. Today, she decides to take a trip to the Museum of Photographic Arts in Balboa Park, where she hopes she can find something interesting to occupy the both of them.

Electronic Touchscreens: Using touch screens, visitors could flip through a narrative that described the history of the photos on display.

Interactive Surfaces: With interactive tabletop surfaces, visitors could interact with digital avatars of photos featured in the exhibit.

Camera Technology: Cameras mounted at the top allowed real-time modification of the photos according to the visitor's appearance.

User Research

  • Contextual Interviews

In order to gain a better idea of the specific needs of their visitors, we conducted research on general museum visitors from all over the state by sending out online surveys and conducting ethnographic interviews with people that we knew. In order to narrow down our target demographic, we decided to focus more strongly on figuring out ways to engage local family populations.


We asked ourselves: What were the goals visitors wanted to achieve when they enter an exhibit? In what ways did people expect to engage with museum galleries?


We started by conducting a physical heuristic evaluation of the museum and contextual inquiries with museum visitors to answer these questions.


Namely, we were interested in (1) how they interacted with exhibits they found interesting, (2) how they navigated to the next display, and (3) who they often engaged with while viewing them.

Personal Devices: Unsurprisingly, lots of visitors used their own devices to take pictures of the exhibit.

Key Findings

  • Technology is the Future

After reviewing the museum demographics and observing visitors that interacted with the exhibits, we discovered several insights about potential users.


Insight 1: Although families often came as an entire group, these visitors were usually split into smaller pairs or left to wander on their own.

Insight 2: Even though visitors were typically not allowed to touch exhibits, they were more intent on creating a memorable experience out of their visit rather than simply take home a souvenir.

Insight 3: In fact, visitors were more likely to visit again if the exhibits, or at least their experiences, held personal relevance to them.


Most importantly, we realized that although visitors harnessed available digital tools to interact with the artwork, they also used their personal devices to create their own unique digital experiences.


Ideation: As a group, we mass-generated a list of possible solutions to our problem, including puzzles to solve, a stamp card, and even an interactive floor that followed visitors as they explored the museum.

Ideation Process

  • Initial Questions

After synthesizing our research into core insights, it was time to start brainstorming solutions. To begin, we asked ourselves four initial questions:

(1) Whether visitors needed to know about MOPA before coming to museum.

(2) Whether our solution would involve the whole family.

(3) If people would be able to take away an experience or souvenir from our solution.

(4) Whether our solution was sustainable and low-budget.


We needed to find a way that would not only bring new interest to the museum, but would also retain repeat visitors willing to visit the museum over upcoming years.


Therefore, in order to ensure that our solution was not only effective, but also sustainable in the long run, we needed to create a prototype that would be cheap and quick to implement.

Q&A Wall: Using augmented reality, readers can see other visitors' answers to the reflection questions written on museum walls.

Polaroid Wall: After uploading personal photos online, visitors can 'post' their pictures onto an empty wall via AR.

Photo Scavenger Hunt: Activities in the app allow visitors to solve a set of puzzles and interactive exercises involving the exhibit.

Ideation Process

  • Parallel Prototyping

In order to explore multiple possibilities in the design space, we narrowed down our list to parallel prototype three different solutions on paper.


Solution 1: A dedicated wall for self-reflection, allowing visitors to answer the questions written on the wall besides the exhibits.

Solution 2: A public board on the wall for posting your own photos, to encourage visitors into participating in the arts.

Solution 3: An exhibit scavenger hunt, to inspire visitors to fully explore the entire museum.

Key Design Decisions

  • Interactivity & Problem-solving

After running several tests at the museum, my team focused on prioritizing key design decisions.


Encouraging collaboration between various family members: With specific activities designed to inspire family members to work together, these AR activities aim to recognize teamwork and collaboration.

Creating a meaningful souvenir to take home: Designed to be introspective and memorable, these printed activities can represent sentimental experiences that arise from that day.


As a result, we ultimately decided to move further with our idea of interactive AR activities.


Photo Unscrambling Activity: In this activity, kids are prompted to solve this photo puzzle with their family members.

Find & Sketch Activity: In this activity, kids are asked to draw in what they think the rest of the photo should look like.

Rapid Prototyping

  • Paper Designs

To quickly test out our prototypes in the field, we created paper activities to emulate the interactivity and collaboration of our intended augmented reality app.


Watching from Afar: The adult is waiting for the child to complete the activity from the other side of the exhibition gallery.

Split Up: While the child is completing the activity alone, the adult is in a different section looking at the artwork.

User Research

  • Field Testing

We printed multiple low-fidelity copies in order to test them, see the results, and start thinking about the next iteration. In total, we asked for four different groups of families that day to test out our prototype and gave them a survey to fill out at the end of their experience.


But we found that what people say... isn't what they do.


Although participants often indicated in the card that they worked with family members on the activities, children and parents were in fact split up for most of their time in the gallery. Many adults resolved to watch over their children from afar, and some pairs were divided up entirely.

Coming Together: While parents and children were often separated during the activities, they did come together at the beginning and at the end to share their findings.

Key Insight

  • Field Testing

Even though the parents, kids, and family members may not have been interacting with one another in the midst of the activities, they did come together at the beginning and at the end to share their experiences.


The activities may not initiate engagement among family members right at MOPA, but they may experience interaction at a different time frame.


Thus, this activity could create an opportunity for the families to facilitate discussion after the trip, whether they may talk about them later in their day.

Museum Navigation: Because there were so many exhibits, we thought that a navigation feature to locate popular photos in the gallery would help families in wayfinding.

Photo Unscramble: Users can point their phone camera towards any photo in the museum and unscramble re-arranged pieces on the screen.

Find & Sketch: With opportunistic blank spaces placed in photos pointed at with a phone, users can draw their own interpretation of what should be in the photo.

Final Designs

  • Hi-fidelity Prototyping

As the team member with the most experience with visual design, I turned our paper prototypes into high-fidelity screens for our proposed AR design. However, due to the lack of technical expertise in our team, we weren't able to implement more than just static screens.


Final Thoughts

  • Designing for Real Users

While it was definitely a challenge designing for a particular audience, this project ultimately kickstarted my passion for turning design-thinking into design-doing. I'm constantly inspired by how perseverance and hard work in a project can ultimately affect real people —and as a result, I'm always searching for more opportunities to dedicate myself towards impactful causes.


Although we haven’t created a museum experience that will allow group members to engage with each other in real-time, we’ve explored a space that will help incentivize families to return to MOPA in the future with hopes of collecting a new activity card, creating discussion and discourse at a later point in time between family members.
