Workday

Contextualizing HR and Finance Experiences

Project Details

  • Summer 2016
  • 12 weeks
  • Product Design

As an intern at Workday, I worked with a total of three developers and three product managers across different teams to complete a total of four projects this summer.


These projects include a data visualization for a waterfall chart, an interactive calculator for web, a wearable Workday app for Android Watch, and a flow for the Google Nearby API on Android phones.


Due to an NDA, I'm only able to talk extensively about my process designing the waterfall chart, which is already live as of Workday's latest update. For more details, you can also contact me at sincerelyvivianlee@gmail.com.

Action: Workday for Android Wear allows users who are busy and constantly on-the-go to complete tasks away from their desks.

Prediction: With the waterfall chart and the interactive calculator, users can make informed decisions about company statistics.

Results: By bringing customers one step closer to their user goals, we can create experiences that are simpler, faster, and more meaningful.

Background

  • Contextual → Actionable Experiences

In Workday's current system, explicit calls-to-action make the dashboard perfect for users who know exactly what they want to do, through the use of dropdown menus, Worklet icons, and the search function.


But what if you're not sure what you should be doing?


After spending some time as an intern here, I felt that we needed to drive towards contextual experiences, where personalized designs that were relevant to a user’s situation could ultimately result in more meaningful product experiences.

Focus Groups: I organized conference rooms for my product manager and I to meet with members of each team, preparing a set of contextual interview questions and asking them to walk us through how they read data visualizations for their tasks.

User Research

  • Focus Groups & User Interviews

Understanding HR and financial professionals: Knowing that we needed a new addition to Workday's data viz library, I reached out to six different teams in Workday to observe their use cases for each visualization, including: Compensation, Revenue (Financials), Human Capital Management, Recruiting, Learning, Workforce Planning.


We found that while Workday's bar charts and stacked column charts provided useful information about static values in an inventory, this information provided little importance for data that often changed over time.

Key Insights

  • Enterprise Data is Detailed & Dynamic

In order to determine the scope for a new data visualization, we distilled the needs we found from research into minimum business requirements:


(1) Because information in HR management was sometimes sensitive to time:

How might we convey changes over time, both miniscule and immense?

(2) Because data analysts typically used information to set future goals:

How might we provide insights that can be generalized to different kinds of data?

(3) Because different factors in an objective can weigh against one another:

How might we compare separate categories that may not be related to each other?


After some thought, we found that designing a waterfall chart for Workday's data viz library would help target all these needs.

Design-Thinking: Weekly design jams provided a space for our developer to give his own input about the waterfall chart.

Design-Doing: Here, our developer, product manager, and I together brainstormed how to work around technical corner cases.

Design Process

  • Whiteboarding

While scoping out the details for the new waterfall chart, I led weekly design jams with my product manager and developer with the intent of bringing more collaboration and design-thinking into the development of this project.


I quickly found that while it was common for designers to participate in brainstorming sessions with each other at Workday, this wasn't typically done between designers, managers, and developers, due to the high amount of projects individuals often worked on at a time.


However, due to my successful experience involving project managers and engineers in participatory design sessions during my past internships, I strongly pushed for these sessions of teamwork and partnership between different disciplines.

User Research

  • Use Cases & Personas

Despite meeting with six different teams, my product manager and I decided to narrow down our use case to a single focus: making changes in budget and finances.


In order to pinpoint the main goals, frustrations, and behaviors that someone for this use case would face, I created a persona based on our interview findings to organize early ideas for how to structure the waterfall chart: focusing on Sara, an accountant at a medium-sized company in California that's doing well to break even —but has the potential to become more profitable.


As a financial analyst, Sara's main goal is to determine which areas her company makes the largest gains and losses, and make cuts to items that will ensure the company makes a marginal profit.


Hover Interactions: My product manager and I worked together to find ways to display information that wasn't immediately visible.

Clustering: I explored different ways to cluster data, such as grouping assets by category and then adding bars after each group.

Hover on Cluster: I designed the ability to hover over individual columns (subcategories) and over entire groups (categories).

Key Design Decisions

  • Hover & Cluster Interactions

According to the persona I developed, frustrations Sara encountered included:


(1) Organizing the many types of assets involved in running a larger company:

From rounds of user interviews, we found that financial professionals who managed assets for bigger entities were faced with many more categories and expenses to organize than those in smaller organizations. Our design would need a more effective way to organize and compare across these expansive categories.


As a result, I designed hover interactions that would allow users to drill down only on the information that they needed.


(2) Comparing these various different categories against each other:

In addition to organizing different categories, professionals needed the ability to compare across items and make the best fit decision for cutting or allocating budgets to categories that make the biggest difference in profit.

Now that we had established our minimum design requirements, it was time to begin designing for these features.


As a result, I designed clustered categories that would not only allow users compare items across different categories —but also group related items (subcategories) within broader categories.


Single Waterfall: In this single waterfall chart, increase and decline are compared across only one category.

Clustered Waterfall: In a clustered waterfall chart, however, changes in value are compared across several different items.

Cluster Hover: Hovering over values in the legend will reveal the totals, increases, and decreases throughout the entire chart.

Category Hover: Hovering over the category name will produce statistics on bars as a group.

Final Thoughts

  • Designing for a Big Company

Ultimately, I learned a lot from my experience interning at Workday. There are a lot of unique challenges that come with designing at a big company —both visual and working (one word: 'JIRA'). I had an incredible time learning tools of the trade and best practices in the industry through the help of my helpful and giving mentor, my patient and intelligent manager, and my talented, understanding team.


In the beginning, I was intimidated by the bureaucracy and workings of a well-established company, having previously interned in small organizations, but I’ve since come to grow so much in my creative confidence and repertoire. It was incredible seeing my project in the works and worthwhile to see it being shipped to real users.
